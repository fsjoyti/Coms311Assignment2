<p><b>Complexity economics</b> is the application of <a href="/wiki/Complex_system" title="Complex system">complexity science</a> to the problems of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>. It sees the economy not as a system in equilibrium, but as one in motion, perpetually constructing itself anew.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Beinhocker_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beinhocker-2">[2]</a></sup> It uses computational rather than mathematical analysis to explore how economic structure is formed and reformed, in continuous interaction with the adaptive behavior of the 'agents' in the economy <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Models">Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complexity_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The "nearly archetypal example" is an artificial stock market model created by the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe_Institute" title="Santa Fe Institute">Santa Fe Institute</a> in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosser1999_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosser1999-4">[4]</a></sup> The model shows two different outcomes, one where "agents do not search much for predictors and there is convergence on a homogeneous rational expectations outcome" and another where "all kinds of technical trading strategies appearing and remaining and periods of bubbles and crashes occurring".<sup id="cite_ref-Rosser1999_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosser1999-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another area has studied the <a href="/wiki/Prisoner%27s_dilemma" title="Prisoner's dilemma">prisoner's dilemma</a>, such as in a network where agents play amongst their nearest neighbors or a network where the agents can make mistakes from time to time and "evolve strategies".<sup id="cite_ref-Rosser1999_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosser1999-4">[4]</a></sup> In these models, the results show a system which displays "a pattern of constantly changing distributions of the strategies".<sup id="cite_ref-Rosser1999_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosser1999-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>More generally, complexity economics models are often used to study how non-intuitive results at the macro-level of a system can emerge from simple interactions at the micro level. This avoids assumptions of the <a href="/wiki/Representative_agent" title="Representative agent">representative agent</a> method, which attributes outcomes in collective systems as the simple sum of the rational actions of the individuals.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measures">Measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complexity_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_Complexity_Index">Economic Complexity Index</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complexity_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economic Complexity Index">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Harvard economist <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Hausmann" title="Ricardo Hausmann">Ricardo Hausmann</a> and MIT physicist Cesar A. Hidalgo introduced a spectral method to measure the complexity of a country's economy by inferring it from the structure of the network connecting countries to the products that they export. The measure combines information of a country's diversity, which is positively correlated with a country's productive knowledge, with measures of a product ubiquity (number of countries that produce or export the product).<sup id="cite_ref-HidalgoHausmannPNAS_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HidalgoHausmannPNAS-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ComplexityAtlas_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComplexityAtlas-6">[6]</a></sup> This concept, known as the <a href="/wiki/The_Product_Space" title="The Product Space">"Product Space"</a>, has been further developed by MIT's <a href="/wiki/The_Observatory_of_Economic_Complexity" title="The Observatory of Economic Complexity">Observatory of Economic Complexity</a>, and in The Atlas of Economic Complexity<sup id="cite_ref-ComplexityAtlas_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComplexityAtlas-6">[6]</a></sup> in 2011.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Relevance">Relevance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complexity_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relevance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Economic_complexity_index" title="Economic complexity index">Economic Complexity Index (ECI)</a> introduced by Hausmann and Hidalgo <sup id="cite_ref-HidalgoHausmannPNAS_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HidalgoHausmannPNAS-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ComplexityAtlas_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComplexityAtlas-6">[6]</a></sup> is highly predictive of future GDP per capita growth. In <sup id="cite_ref-ComplexityAtlas_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComplexityAtlas-6">[6]</a></sup> Hausmann, Hidalgo et al., the authors show that the List of countries by future GDP (based on ECI) estimates ability of the ECI to predict future GDP per capita growth is between 5 times and 20 times larger than the World Bank's measure of governance, the World Economic Forum's (WEF) Global Competitiveness Index (GCI) and standard measures of human capital, such as years of schooling and cognitive ability.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metrics_for_country_fitness_and_product_complexity">Metrics for country fitness and product complexity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complexity_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Metrics for country fitness and product complexity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Luciano_Pietronero" title="Luciano Pietronero">Pietronero</a> and collaborators have recently proposed a different approach.<sup id="cite_ref-metricsfitness1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metricsfitness1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-metricsfitness2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metricsfitness2-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-metricsflyer_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metricsflyer-11">[11]</a></sup> These metrics are defined as the fixed point of non-linear iterative map. Differently from the linear algorithm giving rise to the ECI, this non-linearity is a key point to properly deal with the nested structure of the data. The authors of this alternative formula claim it has several advantages:</p>
<ul>
<li>Consistency with the empirical evidence from the export country-product matrix that diversification plays a crucial role in the assessment of the competitiveness of countries. The metrics for countries proposed by Pietronero is indeed extensive with respect to the number of products.</li>
<li>Non-linear coupling between fitness and complexity required by the nested structure of the country-product matrix. The nested structure implies that the information on the complexity of a product must be bounded by the producers with the slowest fitness.</li>
<li>Broad and <a href="/wiki/Pareto_distribution" title="Pareto distribution">Pareto</a>-like distribution of the metrics.</li>
<li>Each iteration of the method refines information, does not change the meaning of the iterated variables and does not shrink information.</li>
</ul>
<p>The metrics for country fitness and product complexity have been used in a report<sup id="cite_ref-fitnessmtricsBCG_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fitnessmtricsBCG-12">[12]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Consulting_Group" title="Boston Consulting Group">Boston Consulting Group</a> on <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> growth and development perspectives.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complexity_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/W._Brian_Arthur" title="W. Brian Arthur">Brian Arthur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steven_N._Durlauf" class="mw-redirect" title="Steven N. Durlauf">Steven N. Durlauf</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_A._Lane_(scientist)" class="mw-redirect" title="David A. Lane (scientist)">David A. Lane</a> describe several features of complex systems that deserve greater attention in economics.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><b>Dispersed interaction</b>â€”The economy has interaction between many dispersed, heterogeneous, agents. The action of any given agent depends upon the anticipated actions of other agents and on the aggregate state of the economy.</li>
<li><b>No global controller</b>â€”Controls are provided by mechanisms of competition and coordination between agents. Economic actions are mediated by legal institutions, assigned roles, and shifting associations. No global entity controls interactions. Traditionally, a fictitious <i>auctioneer</i> has appeared in some mathematical analyses of general equilibrium models, although nobody claimed any descriptive accuracy for such models. Traditionally, many mainstream models have imposed <i>constraints</i>, such as requiring that budgets be balanced, and such constraints are avoided in complexity economics.</li>
<li><b>Cross-cutting hierarchical organization</b>â€”The economy has many levels of organization and interaction. Units at any given level behaviors, actions, strategies, products typically serve as "building blocks" for constructing units at the next higher level. The overall organization is more than hierarchical, with many sorts of tangling interactions (associations, channels of communication) across levels.</li>
<li><b>Ongoing adaptation</b>â€”Behaviors, actions, strategies, and products are revised frequently as the individual agents accumulate experience.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Novelty niches</b>â€”Such niches are associated with new markets, new technologies, new behaviors, and new institutions. The very act of filling a niche may provide new niches. The result is ongoing novelty.</li>
<li><b>Out-of-equilibrium dynamics</b>â€”Because new niches, new potentials, new possibilities, are continually created, the economy functions without attaining any optimum or global equilibrium. Improvements occur regularly.</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_trends_in_economics">Contemporary trends in economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complexity_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Contemporary trends in economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Complexity economics has a complex relation to previous work in economics and other sciences, and to contemporary economics. Complexity-theoretic thinking to understand economic problems has been present since their <a href="/wiki/Inception" title="Inception">inception</a> as <a href="/wiki/Academic_discipline" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic discipline">academic disciplines</a>. Research has shown that no two separate micro-events are completely isolated,<sup id="cite_ref-AFC-NA-21_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AFC-NA-21-15">[15]</a></sup> and there is a relationship that forms a <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomic" class="mw-redirect" title="Macroeconomic">macroeconomic</a> structure. However, the relationship is not always in one direction; there is a reciprocal influence when feedback is in operation.<sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-06_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-06-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Complexity economics has been applied to many fields.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intellectual_predecessors">Intellectual predecessors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complexity_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Intellectual predecessors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Complexity economics draws inspiration from <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_economics" title="Behavioral economics">behavioral economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marxian_economics" title="Marxian economics">Marxian economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Institutional_economics" title="Institutional economics">institutional economics</a>/<a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_economics" title="Evolutionary economics">evolutionary economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austrian_economics" class="mw-redirect" title="Austrian economics">Austrian economics</a> and the work of <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It also draws inspiration from other fields, such as <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a> in physics, and <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary biology</a>. Some of the 20th century intellectual background of complexity theory in economics is examined in Alan Marshall (2002) The Unity of Nature, Imperial College Press: London.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complexity_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The theory of complex dynamic systems has been applied in diverse fields in economics and other decision sciences. These applications include <a href="/w/index.php?title=Capital_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Capital theory (page does not exist)">capital theory</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Rosser1983_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosser1983-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ahmad1991_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ahmad1991-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Sato2002_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sato2002-20">[20]</a></sup> the dynamics of <a href="/wiki/Public_opinion" title="Public opinion">opinions</a> among agents composed of multiple selves,<sup id="cite_ref-Krause2010_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krause2010-21">[21]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomic" class="mw-redirect" title="Macroeconomic">macroeconomics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Flaschel2009_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Flaschel2009-22">[22]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Voting_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Voting theory">voting theory</a>, the methods of <a href="/wiki/Symbolic_dynamics" title="Symbolic dynamics">symbolic dynamics</a> have been applied by <a href="/wiki/Donald_G._Saari" title="Donald G. Saari">Donald G. Saari</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Saari2001_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saari2001-23">[23]</a></sup> Complexity economics has attracted the attention of historians of economics.<sup id="cite_ref-Bausor1994_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bausor1994-24">[24]</a></sup> Ben Ramalingam's <a href="/wiki/Aid_on_the_Edge_of_Chaos" title="Aid on the Edge of Chaos">Aid on the Edge of Chaos</a> includes numerous applications of complexity economics that are relevant to foreign <a href="/wiki/Aid" title="Aid">aid</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Complexity_economics_as_mainstream.2C_but_non-orthodox">Complexity economics as mainstream, but non-orthodox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complexity_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Complexity economics as mainstream, but non-orthodox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="#CITEREFColander2000">Colander (2000)</a>, <a href="#CITEREFColanderHoltRosser2004">Colander, Holt &amp; Rosser (2004)</a>, and <a href="#CITEREFDavis2008">Davis (2008)</a> contemporary <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_economics" title="Mainstream economics">mainstream economics</a> is evolving to be more "eclectic",<sup id="cite_ref-COL2000a_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COL2000a-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-COL2000b_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-COL2000b-26">[26]</a></sup> diverse,<sup id="cite_ref-CHR04a_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHR04a-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CHR04b_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHR04b-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CHR04c_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHR04c-29">[29]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pluralism_in_economics" title="Pluralism in economics">pluralistic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DAV2008a_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAV2008a-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="#CITEREFColanderHoltRosser2004">Colander, Holt &amp; Rosser (2004)</a> state that contemporary mainstream economics is "moving away from a strict adherence to the holy trinity---rationality, selfishness, and equilibrium", citing complexity economics along with <a href="/wiki/Recursive_economics" title="Recursive economics">recursive economics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamical systems</a> as contributions to these trends.<sup id="cite_ref-CHR04d_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHR04d-31">[31]</a></sup> They classify complexity economics as now mainstream but <a href="/wiki/Heterodox_economics" title="Heterodox economics">non-orthodox</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CHR04e_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHR04e-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DAV2008b_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAV2008b-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complexity_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1995-1997 publications, <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i> journalist John Horgan "ridiculed" the movement as being the fourth <i>C</i> among the "failed fads" of "<a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complexity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catastrophe_theory" title="Catastrophe theory">catastrophe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">cybernetics</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Rosser1999_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosser1999-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1997, Horgan wrote that the approach had "created some potent metaphors: the butterfly effect, fractals, artificial life, the edge of chaos, self organized criticality. But they have not told us anything about the world that is both concrete and truly surprising, either in a negative or in a positive sense".<sup id="cite_ref-Rosser1999_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosser1999-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> However, most of Horgan's criticisms are now considered as both inaccurate and outdated.</p>
<p>Rosser "granted" Horgan "that it is hard to identify a concrete and surprising discovery (rather than "mere metaphor") that has arisen due to the emergence of complexity analysis" in the discussion journal of the <a href="/wiki/American_Economic_Association" title="American Economic Association">American Economic Association</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Economic_Perspectives" title="Journal of Economic Perspectives">Journal of Economic Perspectives</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosser1999_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosser1999-4">[4]</a></sup> Surveying economic studies based on complexity science, Rosser wrote that the findings, rather than being surprising, confirmed "already-observed facts".<sup id="cite_ref-Rosser1999_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosser1999-4">[4]</a></sup> Rosser wrote that there has been "little work on empirical techniques for testing dispersed&#160;agent complexity&#160;models".<sup id="cite_ref-Rosser1999_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosser1999-4">[4]</a></sup> Nonetheless, Rosser wrote that "there is a strain of common perspective that has been accumulating as the four C's of cybernetics, catastrophe, chaos and complexity emerged, which may now be reaching a critical mass in terms of influencing the thinking of economists more broadly".<sup id="cite_ref-Rosser1999_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosser1999-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
