title="Object (philosophy)">objects</a>, i.e. ideas or concepts. In this context a <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> is taken to mean "an interrelated, interworking set of objects".</p><div id="toc" class="toc"><h2>Contents</h2><ul><ul></ul><li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a><li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-4"><a href="#Concept"><span class="tocnumber">2.1</span> <span class="toctext">Concept</span></a></li><li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-6"><a href="#Conceptual_framework"><span class="tocnumber">2.3</span> <span class="toctext">Conceptual framework</span></a></li></li><li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-8"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li><li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-10"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li></div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conceptual_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>Broadly, when a conceptual system includes a range of values, ideas, and beliefs the conceptual system is said be a <a href="/wiki/Worldview" class="mw-redirect" title="Worldview">view of the world</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_work" title="Social work">social work</a>, a conceptual system may refer to an individual's <a href="/wiki/Mental_model" title="Mental model">mental model</a> of the world. In humans, a conceptual system may be understood as kind of a <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> for the world.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In science, there are many forms of conceptual systems including laws, theories, and models. Those conceptual systems may be developed through <a href="/wiki/Inductive_reasoning" title="Inductive reasoning">inductive reasoning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deductive_reasoning" title="Deductive reasoning">deductive reasoning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Empirical_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Empirical analysis">empirical analysis</a>.</p><p>Within the academic literature, each theory may be understood as a conceptual system. Conceptual systems are generally held to have greater value when they are more useful, based on more research, and are more systemically interrelated.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conceptual_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>A <b>concept</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Abstraction" title="Abstraction">abstract</a> <a href="/wiki/Idea" title="Idea">idea</a> or a mental symbol, typically associated with a corresponding representation in <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> or <a href="/wiki/Symbology" class="mw-redirect" title="Symbology">symbology</a>, that denotes all of the objects in a given <a href="/wiki/Categories_(Aristotle)" title="Categories (Aristotle)">category</a> or <a href="/wiki/Class_(philosophy)" title="Class (philosophy)">class</a> of entities, <a href="/wiki/Interaction" title="Interaction">interactions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenomena" class="mw-redirect" title="Phenomena">phenomena</a>, or relationships between them. Concepts are abstract in that they omit the <a href="/wiki/Difference_(philosophy)" title="Difference (philosophy)">differences</a> of the things in <a href="/wiki/Extension_(semantics)" title="Extension (semantics)">extension</a>, treating them as if they were identical. They are universal in that they apply equally to everything in their extension. Concepts are also the basic elements of <a href="/wiki/Proposition" title="Proposition">propositions</a>, much the same way a <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">word</a> is the basic <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantic</a> element of a <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">sentence</a>. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perceptions</a>, which are particular images of individual objects, concepts cannot be visualized. Because they are not, themselves, individual perceptions, concepts are discursive and result from <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a>. They can only be thought about, or designated, by means of a <a href="/wiki/Name" title="Name">name</a>. Words are not concepts. Words are signs for concepts.</p><div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_schema" title="Conceptual schema">Conceptual schema</a></div><p>For example, a stochastic model of stock prices includes in its ontology a sample space, random variables, the mean and variance of stock prices, various regression coefficients etc. Models of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Pure_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Pure state">pure states</a> are represented as unit vectors in a Hilbert space include in their ontologies <a href="/wiki/Observable" title="Observable">observables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">dynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a> operators etc. It is possible that observables and states of quantum mechanics are as physically real as the electrons they model, but by adopting this purely formal notion of ontology we avoid altogether this question.</p><div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_framework" title="Conceptual framework">Conceptual framework</a></div><p>For example, a conceptual framework of accounting "seeks to identify the nature, subject, purpose and broad content of general-purpose financial reporting and the qualitative characteristics that financial information should possess".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><div role="navigation" aria-label="Portals" class="noprint
