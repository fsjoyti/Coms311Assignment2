				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">This article discusses the application of mathematical models to capture emergent properties of complex physical systems. For other scientific and professional disciplines addressing complexity in their fields, see <a href="/wiki/Complex_systems" title="Complex systems">complex systems</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>complex system</b> is a <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> composed of many components which may interact with each other. In many cases it is useful to represent such a system as a network where the nodes represent the components and the links their interactions. Examples of complex systems are Earth's global <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisms">organisms</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">human brain</a>, social organization, an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>, a living <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a>, and ultimately the entire <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>.</p>
=======
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>A <b>complex adaptive system</b> is a "complex macroscopic collection" of relatively "similar and partially connected micro-structures" formed in order to <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_system" title="Adaptive system">adapt</a> to the changing environment and increase its survivability as a <a href="/wiki/Macrostructure_(sociology)" title="Macrostructure (sociology)">macro-structure</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-01_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-01-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-02_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-02-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-12_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-12-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>They are <i><a href="/wiki/Complex_system" title="Complex system">complex</a></i> in that they are <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_network_analysis" title="Dynamic network analysis">dynamic networks of interactions</a>, and their relationships are not aggregations of the individual static entities, i.e., the behavior of the ensemble is not predicted by the behavior of the components. They are <i><a href="/wiki/Adaptive" class="mw-redirect" title="Adaptive">adaptive</a></i> in that the individual and <a href="/wiki/Collective_behavior" title="Collective behavior">collective behavior</a> mutate and <a href="/wiki/Self-organizing" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-organizing">self-organize</a> corresponding to the change-initiating micro-event or collection of events.<sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-01_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-01-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-02_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-02-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This field is highly interdisciplinary, and blends insights from the natural and social sciences to develop system-level models and insights that allow for <a href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_agents" class="mw-redirect" title="Heterogeneous agents">heterogeneous agents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transition</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Emergent_behavior" class="mw-redirect" title="Emergent behavior">emergent behavior</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complex_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although it is arguable that humans have been studying complex systems for thousands of years, the modern scientific study of complex systems is relatively young in comparison to established fields of science such as <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>. The history of the scientific study of these systems follows several different research trends.</p>
<p>In the area of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, arguably the largest contribution to the study of complex systems was the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Deterministic" class="mw-redirect" title="Deterministic">deterministic</a> systems, a feature of certain <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Dynamical systems">dynamical systems</a> that is strongly related to <a href="/wiki/Nonlinearity" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonlinearity">nonlinearity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The study of <a href="/wiki/Neural_networks" class="mw-redirect" title="Neural networks">neural networks</a> was also integral in advancing the mathematics needed to study complex systems.</p>
<p>The notion of <a href="/wiki/Self-organizing" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-organizing">self-organizing</a> systems is tied up to work in <a href="/wiki/Nonequilibrium_thermodynamics" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonequilibrium thermodynamics">nonequilibrium thermodynamics</a>, including that pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nobel_laureate" class="mw-redirect" title="Nobel laureate">Nobel laureate</a> <a href="/wiki/Ilya_Prigogine" title="Ilya Prigogine">Ilya Prigogine</a> in his study of <a href="/wiki/Dissipative_structures" class="mw-redirect" title="Dissipative structures">dissipative structures</a>. Even older is the work by <a href="/wiki/Hartree-Fock" class="mw-redirect" title="Hartree-Fock">Hartree-Fock</a> c.s. on the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum-chemistry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Quantum-chemistry (page does not exist)">quantum-chemistry</a> equations and later calculations of the structure of molecules which can be regarded as one of the earliest examples of emergence and emergent wholes in science.</p>
<p>The first research institute focused on complex systems, the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe_Institute" title="Santa Fe Institute">Santa Fe Institute</a>, was founded in 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Early Santa Fe Institute participants included physics Nobel laureates <a href="/wiki/Murray_Gell-Mann" title="Murray Gell-Mann">Murray Gell-Mann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_Warren_Anderson" title="Philip Warren Anderson">Philip Anderson</a>, economics Nobel laureate <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Arrow" title="Kenneth Arrow">Kenneth Arrow</a>, and Manhattan Project scientists <a href="/wiki/George_Cowan" title="George Cowan">George Cowan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herbert_L._Anderson" title="Herbert L. Anderson">Herb Anderson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Today, there are over 50 <a href="/wiki/Complex_systems#Institutes_and_research_centers" title="Complex systems">institutes and research centers</a> focusing on complex systems.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complex_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonlinear_systems">Nonlinear systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complex_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nonlinear systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The behaviour of non-linear systems is not subject to the principle of <a href="/wiki/Superposition_principle" title="Superposition principle">superposition</a> while that of <a href="/wiki/Linear_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Linear systems">linear systems</a> is subject to superposition. Thus, a complex <a href="/wiki/Nonlinearity" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonlinearity">nonlinear system</a> is one whose behaviour cannot be expressed as a sum of the behaviour of its parts (or of their multiples).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<li>it must be <a href="/wiki/Butterfly_effect" title="Butterfly effect">sensitive to initial conditions</a>,</li>
<li>it must be <a href="/wiki/Topological_mixing" class="mw-redirect" title="Topological mixing">topologically mixing</a>, and</li>
<li>its <a href="/wiki/Periodic_orbit" class="mw-redirect" title="Periodic orbit">periodic orbits</a> must be <a href="/wiki/Dense_set" title="Dense set">dense</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>Sensitivity to <a href="/wiki/Initial_condition" title="Initial condition">initial conditions</a> means that each point in such a system is arbitrarily closely approximated by other points with significantly different future <a href="/wiki/Phase_space" title="Phase space">trajectories</a>. Thus, an arbitrarily small perturbation of the current trajectory may lead to significantly different future behavior.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Complex_adaptive_systems">Complex adaptive systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complex_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Complex adaptive systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Complex_adaptive_system" title="Complex adaptive system">Complex adaptive systems</a> (CAS) are special cases of complex systems. They are <a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complex</a> in that they are diverse and made up of multiple interconnected elements and <a href="/wiki/Adaptive" class="mw-redirect" title="Adaptive">adaptive</a> in that they have the capacity to change and learn from experience. Examples of complex adaptive systems include the <a href="/wiki/Stock_market" title="Stock market">stock market</a>, social insect and <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ant</a> colonies, the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">brain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a> and the developing <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing businesses</a> and any human social group-based endeavor in a cultural and <a href="/wiki/Social_system" title="Social system">social system</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political parties</a> or <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">communities</a>. This includes some large-scale online systems, such as collaborative tagging or <a href="/wiki/Social_bookmarking" title="Social bookmarking">social bookmarking</a> systems.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complex_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Complex systems may have the following features:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Cascading_failure" title="Cascading failure">Cascading failures</a></dt>
<dd>Due to the strong coupling between components in complex systems, a failure in one or more components can lead to cascading failures which may have catastrophic consequences on the functioning of the system.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Complex systems may be open</dt>
<dd>Complex systems are usually <a href="/wiki/Open_system_(systems_theory)" title="Open system (systems theory)">open systems</a> â€” that is, they exist in a <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermodynamic">thermodynamic</a> gradient and dissipate energy. In other words, complex systems are frequently far from energetic <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">equilibrium</a>: but despite this flux, there may be <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pattern_stability&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pattern stability (page does not exist)">pattern stability</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Synergetics_(Haken)" title="Synergetics (Haken)">synergetics</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Complex systems may have a memory</dt>
<dd>The history of a complex system may be important. Because complex systems are <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Dynamical systems">dynamical systems</a> they change over time, and prior states may have an influence on present states. More formally, complex systems often exhibit <a href="/wiki/Hysteresis" title="Hysteresis">hysteresis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MajdandzicPodobnik2013_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MajdandzicPodobnik2013-8">[8]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Complex systems may be <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy#Nested_hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">nested</a></dt>
<dd>The components of a complex system may themselves be complex systems. For example, an <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economy</a> is made up of <a href="/wiki/Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisation">organisations</a>, which are made up of <a href="/wiki/Person" title="Person">people</a>, which are made up of <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> - all of which are complex systems.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Dynamic network of multiplicity</dt>
<dd>As well as <a href="/wiki/Coupling" title="Coupling">coupling</a> rules, the dynamic <a href="/wiki/Biological_network" title="Biological network">network</a> of a complex system is important. <a href="/wiki/Small-world_network" title="Small-world network">Small-world</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scale-free_network" title="Scale-free network">scale-free</a> networks<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which have many local interactions and a smaller number of inter-area connections are often employed. Natural complex systems often exhibit such topologies. In the human <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cortex</a> for example, we see dense local connectivity and a few very long <a href="/wiki/Axonal" class="mw-redirect" title="Axonal">axon</a> projections between regions inside the cortex and to other brain regions.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>May produce emergent phenomena</dt>
<dd>Complex systems may exhibit behaviors that are <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergent</a>, which is to say that while the results may be sufficiently determined by the activity of the systems' basic constituents, they may have properties that can only be studied at a higher level. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Termites" class="mw-redirect" title="Termites">termites</a> in a mound have physiology, biochemistry and biological development that are at one level of analysis, but their <a href="/wiki/Social_behavior" title="Social behavior">social behavior</a> and mound building is a property that emerges from the collection of termites and needs to be analysed at a different level.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Relationships are non-linear</dt>
<dd>In practical terms, this means a small perturbation may cause a large effect (see <a href="/wiki/Butterfly_effect" title="Butterfly effect">butterfly effect</a>), a proportional effect, or even no effect at all. In linear systems, effect is <i>always</i> directly proportional to cause. See <a href="/wiki/Nonlinearity" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonlinearity">nonlinearity</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Relationships contain feedback loops</dt>
<dd>Both negative (<a href="/wiki/Damping" class="mw-redirect" title="Damping">damping</a>) and positive (amplifying) <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a> are always found in complex systems. The effects of an element's behaviour are fed back to in such a way that the element itself is altered.</dd>
</dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complex_adaptive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>complex adaptive systems</i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Complexity_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Complexity science">complexity science</a></i>, is often used to describe the loosely organized academic field that has grown up around the study of such systems. Complexity science is not a single theoryâ€”it encompasses more than one theoretical framework and is highly interdisciplinary, seeking the answers to some fundamental questions about <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">living</a>, adaptable, changeable systems. The study of CAS focuses on complex, emergent and macroscopic properties of the system.<sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-12_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-12-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-11_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-11-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-13_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-13-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Henry_Holland" title="John Henry Holland">John H. Holland</a> said that CAS "are systems that have a large numbers of components, often called agents, that interact and adapt or learn."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Typical examples of complex adaptive systems include: cities; firms; markets; governments; industries; ecosystems; social networks; power grids; animal swarms; traffic flows; <a href="/wiki/Social_insect" class="mw-redirect" title="Social insect">social insect</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ant</a>) colonies;<sup id="cite_ref-AFC-NA-21_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AFC-NA-21-9">[9]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a> and the developing <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a>. Human social group-based endeavors, such as <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political parties</a>, <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">communities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geopolitical" class="mw-redirect" title="Geopolitical">geopolitical</a> <a href="/wiki/Organisations" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisations">organizations</a>, <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Terrorist_network_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Terrorist network analysis">terrorist networks</a> are also considered CAS.<sup id="cite_ref-AFC-NA-21_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AFC-NA-21-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GT-33_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GT-33-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-19_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-19-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyberspace" title="Cyberspace">cyberspace</a>â€”composed, collaborated, and managed by a complex mix of <a href="/wiki/Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction" title="Humanâ€“computer interaction">humanâ€“computer interactions</a>, is also regarded as a complex adaptive system.<sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-16_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-16-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-17_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-17-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CAS-T-18_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAS-T-18-14">[14]</a></sup> CAS can be hierarchaical, but more often exhibit aspects of "self-orgnaization."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_properties">General properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complex_adaptive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>What distinguishes a CAS from a pure <a href="/wiki/Multi-agent_system" title="Multi-agent system">multi-agent system</a> (MAS) is the focus on top-level properties and features like <a href="/wiki/Self-similarity" title="Self-similarity">self-similarity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complexity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Self-organization" title="Self-organization">self-organization</a>. A MAS is defined as a system composed of multiple interacting agents; whereas in CAS, the agents as well as the system are adaptive and the system is <a href="/wiki/Self-similar" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-similar">self-similar</a>. A CAS is a complex, self-similar collectivity of interacting, adaptive agents. Complex Adaptive Systems are characterized by a high degree of <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_capacity" title="Adaptive capacity">adaptive capacity</a>, giving them resilience in the face of <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/perturbation" class="extiw" title="wikt:perturbation">perturbation</a>.</p>
<p>Other important properties are adaptation (or <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>), communication, cooperation, specialization, spatial and temporal organization, and reproduction. They can be found on all levels: cells specialize, adapt and reproduce themselves just like larger organisms do. Communication and cooperation take place on all levels, from the agent to the system level. The forces driving <a href="/wiki/Co-operation" class="mw-redirect" title="Co-operation">co-operation</a> between agents in such a system, in some cases, can be analyzed with <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complex_adaptive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some of the most important characteristics of complex systems are:<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The number of elements is sufficiently large that conventional descriptions (e.g. a system of <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a>) are not only impractical, but cease to assist in understanding the system. Moreover, the elements interact dynamically, and the interactions can be physical or involve the exchange of information</li>
<li>Such interactions are rich, i.e. any element or sub-system in the system is affected by and affects several other elements or sub-systems</li>
<li>The interactions are <a href="/wiki/Non-linear" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-linear">non-linear</a>: small changes in inputs, physical interactions or stimuli can cause large effects or very significant changes in outputs</li>
<li>Interactions are primarily but not exclusively with immediate neighbours and the nature of the influence is modulated</li>
<li>Any interaction can feed back onto itself directly or after a number of intervening stages. Such feedback can vary in quality. This is known as <i>recurrency</i></li>
<li>The overall behavior of the system of elements is not predicted by the behavior of the individual elements</li>
<li>Such systems may be open and it may be difficult or impossible to define system boundaries</li>
<li>Complex systems operate under <a href="/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics" title="Non-equilibrium thermodynamics">far from equilibrium</a> conditions. There has to be a constant flow of energy to maintain the organization of the system</li>
<li>Complex systems have a history. They evolve and their past is co-responsible for their present behaviour</li>
<li>Elements in the system may be ignorant of the behaviour of the system as a whole, responding only to the information or physical stimuli available to them locally</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Axelrod" title="Robert Axelrod">Robert Axelrod</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Cohen_(academic)" title="Michael D. Cohen (academic)">Michael D. Cohen</a><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> identify a series of key terms from a modeling perspective:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Strategy</b>, a conditional action pattern that indicates what to do in which circumstances</li>
<li><b>Artifact</b>, a material resource that has definite location and can respond to the action of agents</li>
<li><b>Agent</b>, a collection of properties, strategies &amp; capabilities for interacting with artifacts &amp; other agents</li>
<li><b>Population</b>, a collection of agents, or, in some situations, collections of strategies</li>
<li><b>System</b>, a larger collection, including one or more populations of agents and possibly also artifacts</li>
<li><b>Type</b>, all the agents (or strategies) in a population that have some characteristic in common</li>
<li><b>Variety</b>, the diversity of types within a population or system</li>
<li><b>Interaction pattern</b>, the recurring regularities of contact among types within a system</li>
<li><b>Space (physical)</b>, location in geographical space &amp; time of agents and artifacts</li>
<li><b>Space (conceptual)</b>, "location" in a set of categories structured so that "nearby" agents will tend to interact</li>
<li><b>Selection</b>, processes that lead to an increase or decrease in the frequency of various types of agent or strategies</li>
<li><b>Success criteria</b> or <b>performance measures</b>, a "score" used by an agent or designer in attributing credit in the selection of relatively successful (or unsuccessful) strategies or agents</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modeling_and_simulation">Modeling and simulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complex_adaptive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modeling and simulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CAS are occasionally modeled by means of <a href="/wiki/Agent-based_model" title="Agent-based model">agent-based models</a> and <a href="/wiki/Complex_network" title="Complex network">complex network</a>-based models.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Agent-based models are developed by means of various methods and tools primarily by means of first identifying the different agents inside the model.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Another method of developing models for CAS involves developing complex network models by means of using interaction data of various CAS components.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/SpringerOpen" class="mw-redirect" title="SpringerOpen">SpringerOpen/BioMed Central</a> has launched an online open-access journal on the topic of <i>complex adaptive systems modeling</i> (CASM).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_complexity">Evolution of complexity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Complex_adaptive_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evolution of complexity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Evolution_of_complexity.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Evolution_of_complexity.svg/300px-Evolution_of_complexity.svg.png" width="300" height="262" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Evolution_of_complexity.svg/450px-Evolution_of_complexity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Evolution_of_complexity.svg/600px-Evolution_of_complexity.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1665" data-file-height="1452" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Evolution_of_complexity.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Passive versus active trends in the evolution of complexity. CAS at the beginning of the processes are colored red. Changes in the number of systems are shown by the height of the bars, with each set of graphs moving up in a time series.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Evolution_of_biological_complexity" title="Evolution of biological complexity">Evolution of biological complexity</a></div>
<p>Living organisms are complex adaptive systems. Although complexity is hard to quantify in biology, <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> has produced some remarkably complex organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This observation has led to the common misconception of evolution being progressive and leading towards what are viewed as "higher organisms".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>If this were generally true, evolution would possess an active trend towards complexity. As shown below, in this type of process the value of the most common amount of complexity would increase over time.<sup id="cite_ref-Carroll_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll-24">[24]</a></sup> Indeed, some <a href="/wiki/Artificial_life" title="Artificial life">artificial life</a> simulations have suggested that the generation of CAS is an inescapable feature of evolution.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, the idea of a general trend towards complexity in evolution can also be explained through a passive process.<sup id="cite_ref-Carroll_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll-24">[24]</a></sup> This involves an increase in <a href="/wiki/Variance" title="Variance">variance</a> but the most common value, the <a href="/wiki/Mode_(statistics)" title="Mode (statistics)">mode</a>, does not change. Thus, the maximum level of complexity increases over time, but only as an indirect product of there being more organisms in total. This type of random process is also called a bounded <a href="/wiki/Random_walk" title="Random walk">random walk</a>.</p>
<p>In this hypothesis, the apparent trend towards more complex organisms is an illusion resulting from concentrating on the small number of large, very complex organisms that inhabit the <a href="/wiki/Skewness" title="Skewness">right-hand tail</a> of the complexity distribution and ignoring simpler and much more common organisms. This passive model emphasizes that the overwhelming majority of species are <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microscopic</a> <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotes</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> which comprise about half the world's <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and constitute the vast majority of Earth's biodiversity.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Therefore, simple life remains dominant on Earth, and complex life appears more diverse only because of <a href="/wiki/Sampling_bias" title="Sampling bias">sampling bias</a>.</p>
<p>This lack of an overall trend towards complexity in biology does not preclude the existence of forces driving systems towards complexity in a subset of cases. These minor trends are balanced by other evolutionary pressures that drive systems towards less complex states.</p>
