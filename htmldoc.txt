<p><b>Karl Ludwig von Bertalanffy</b> (September 19, 1901 â€“ June 12, 1972) was an <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biologist</a> known as one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/Systems_theory" title="Systems theory">general systems theory</a> (GST). This is an interdisciplinary practice that describes systems with interacting components, applicable to <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">cybernetics</a> and other fields. Bertalanffy proposed that the classical laws of thermodynamics applied to closed systems, but not necessarily to "open systems," such as living things. His mathematical model of an organism's growth over time, published in 1934, is still in use today.<sup id="cite_ref-ber1934_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ber1934-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bertalanffy grew up in Austria and subsequently worked in Vienna, London, Canada, and the USA.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_von_Bertalanffy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ludwig von Bertalanffy was born and grew up in the little village of Atzgersdorf (now <a href="/wiki/Liesing" title="Liesing">Liesing</a>) near <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. The Bertalanffy family had roots in the 16th century nobility of <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> which included several scholars and court officials.<sup id="cite_ref-TEW89_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TEW89-2">[2]</a></sup> His grandfather Charles Joseph von Bertalanffy (1833â€“1912) had settled in Austria and was a state theatre director in <a href="/wiki/Klagenfurt" title="Klagenfurt">Klagenfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a> and Vienna, which were important sites in imperial Austria. Ludwig's father Gustav von Bertalanffy (1861â€“1919) was a prominent <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">railway</a> <a href="/wiki/Administration_(business)" class="mw-redirect" title="Administration (business)">administrator</a>. On his mother's side Ludwig's grandfather Joseph Vogel was an imperial counsellor and a wealthy Vienna publisher. Ludwig's mother Charlotte Vogel was seventeen when she married the thirty-four-year-old Gustav. They divorced when Ludwig was ten, and both remarried outside the Catholic Church in civil ceremonies.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ludwig von Bertalanffy grew up as an only child educated at home by private tutors until he was ten. When he arrived at his <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(Germany)" title="Gymnasium (Germany)">Gymnasium</a> (a form of grammar school) he was already well habituated in learning by reading, and he continued to study on his own. His neighbour, the famous biologist <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kammerer" title="Paul Kammerer">Paul Kammerer</a>, became a mentor and an example to the young Ludwig.<sup id="cite_ref-bertalanffy.org_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bertalanffy.org-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1918 he started his studies at the university level in philosophy and art history, first at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Innsbruck" title="University of Innsbruck">University of Innsbruck</a> and then at the University of Vienna. Ultimately, Bertalanffy had to make a choice between studying <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">philosophy of science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, and chose the latter because, according to him, one could always become a philosopher later, but not a biologist. In 1926 he finished his PhD thesis (translated title: "Fechner and the problem of integration of the higher order") on the psychologist and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Theodor_Fechner" class="mw-redirect" title="Gustav Theodor Fechner">Gustav Theodor Fechner</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bertalanffy.org_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bertalanffy.org-4">[4]</a></sup> For the next six years he concentrated on a project of â€œtheoretical biologyâ€� whichfocused on the philosophy of biology. He received his <a href="/wiki/Habilitation" title="Habilitation">habilitation</a> in 1934 in â€œtheoretical biologyâ€�.<sup id="cite_ref-DAP_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAP-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bertalanffy was appointed <a href="/wiki/Privatdozent" title="Privatdozent">Privatdozent</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Vienna" title="University of Vienna">University of Vienna</a> in 1934. The post yielded little income and Bertalanffy faced continuing financial difficulties. He applied for promotion to the status of Associate Professor, but the opportunity of funding from the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation" title="Rockefeller Foundation">Rockefeller Foundation</a> enabled him to make a trip to <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> in 1937 to work with <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Rashevsky" title="Nicolas Rashevsky">Nicolas Rashevsky</a> there and also visiting the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Biological_Laboratory" title="Marine Biological Laboratory">Marine Biological Laboratory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DAP_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAP-5">[5]</a></sup> He was still in the US when he heard of the <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">Anschluss</a> in March 1938. However his attempts to remain in the USA failed and he returned to Vienna in October that year.<sup id="cite_ref-DAP_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAP-5">[5]</a></sup> Within a month of his return, he joined the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> which helped his promotion to professor at the University of Vienna in 1940.<sup id="cite_ref-DAP_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAP-5">[5]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> he linked his â€œorganismicâ€� philosophy of biology to the dominant Nazi ideology, principally that of the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BChrerprinzip" title="FÃ¼hrerprinzip">FÃ¼hrerprinzip</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DAP_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAP-5">[5]</a></sup> Following the defeat of Nazism, he found <a href="/wiki/Denazification" title="Denazification">denazification</a> problematic and left Vienna in 1948. He moved to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a> (1948â€“49), the <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_de_Montr%C3%A9al" title="UniversitÃ© de MontrÃ©al">UniversitÃ© de MontrÃ©al</a> (1949), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ottawa" title="University of Ottawa">University of Ottawa</a> (1950â€“54), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a> (1955â€“58), the <a href="/wiki/Menninger_Foundation" title="Menninger Foundation">Menninger Foundation</a> (1958â€“60), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alberta" title="University of Alberta">University of Alberta</a> (1961â€“68), and the <a href="/wiki/University_at_Buffalo,_The_State_University_of_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="University at Buffalo, The State University of New York">State University of New York at Buffalo</a> (SUNY) (1969â€“72).</p>
<p>In 1972, he died from a sudden <a href="/wiki/Heart_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_life">Family life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_von_Bertalanffy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bertalanffy met his future wife, Maria, in April 1924 in the Austrian Alps, and were almost never apart for the next forty-eight years.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> She wanted to finish studying but never did, instead devoting her life to Bertalanffy's career. Later in Canada she would work both for him and with him in his career, and after his death she compiled two of Bertalanffy's last works. They had one child, a son who would follow in his father's footsteps by making his profession in the field of cancer research.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_von_Bertalanffy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today, Bertalanffy is considered to be a founder and one of the principal authors of the interdisciplinary school of thought known as <a href="/wiki/General_systems_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="General systems theory">general systems theory</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Thaddus_E._Weckowicz" title="Thaddus E. Weckowicz">Weckowicz</a> (1989), he "occupies an important position in the intellectual history of the twentieth century. His contributions went beyond <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, and extended into <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">cybernetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">psychiatry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>. Some of his admirers even believe that this theory will one day provide a conceptual framework for all these disciplines".<sup id="cite_ref-TEW89_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TEW89-2">[2]</a></sup> Spending most of his life in semi-obscurity, Ludwig von Bertalanffy may well be the least known intellectual titan of the twentieth century.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Individual_growth_model">Individual growth model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_von_Bertalanffy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Individual growth model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_system_theory">General system theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_von_Bertalanffy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: General system theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The biologist is widely recognized for his contributions to science as a systems theorist; specifically, for the development of a theory known as <a href="/wiki/Systems_theory" title="Systems theory">general system theory</a> (GST). The theory attempted to provide alternatives to conventional models of <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">organization</a>. GST defined new foundations and developments as a generalized theory of systems with applications to numerous areas of study, emphasizing <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holism</a> over reductionism, <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> over mechanism.</p>
<p>Foundational to GST are the inter-relationships between elements which all together form the whole.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_systems">Open systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_von_Bertalanffy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Open systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Open_system_(systems_theory)" title="Open system (systems theory)">Open system (systems theory)</a></div>
<p>Bertalanffy's contribution to systems theory is best known for his theory of open systems. The system theorist argued that traditional <a href="/wiki/Closed_system" title="Closed system">closed system</a> models based on classical science and the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a> were inadequate for explaining large classes of phenomena. Bertalanffy maintained that â€œthe conventional formulation of physics are, in principle, inapplicable to the living organism being open system having <a href="/wiki/Steady_state" title="Steady state">steady state</a>. We may well suspect that many characteristics of living systems which are paradoxical in view of the laws of physics are a consequence of this fact.â€� <sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, while closed <a href="/wiki/Physical_system" title="Physical system">physical systems</a> were questioned, questions equally remained over whether or not open physical systems could justifiably lead to a definitive science for the application of an open systems view to a general theory of systems.</p>
<p>In Bertalanffyâ€™s model, the theorist defined general principles of open systems and the limitations of conventional models. He ascribed applications to biology, <a href="/wiki/Information_theory" title="Information theory">information theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">cybernetics</a>. Concerning <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, examples from the open systems view suggested they â€œmay suffice to indicate briefly the large fields of <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">application</a>â€� that could be the â€œoutlines of a wider <a href="/wiki/Generalization" title="Generalization">generalization</a>;â€� <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> from which, a <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a> for cybernetics. Although potential applications exist in other areas, the theorist developed only the implications for biology and cybernetics. Bertalanffy also noted unsolved problems, which included continued questions over thermodynamics, thus the unsubstantiated claim that there are <a href="/wiki/Physical_law" title="Physical law">physical laws</a> to support generalizations (particularly for information theory), and the need for further research into the problems and potential with the applications of the open system view from physics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Systems_in_the_social_sciences">Systems in the social sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_von_Bertalanffy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Systems in the social sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a>, Bertalanffy did believe that general systems concepts were applicable, e.g. theories that had been introduced into the field of <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> from a modern systems approach that included â€œthe concept of general system, of <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>, etc.â€� <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The theorist critiqued classical â€œatomisticâ€� conceptions of social systems and ideation â€œsuch as â€˜social physicsâ€™ as was often attempted in a reductionist spirit.â€� <sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Bertalanffy also recognized difficulties with the application of a new general theory to social science due to the complexity of the intersections between natural sciences and human social systems. However, the theory still encouraged for new developments from sociology, to <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> among other areas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="would be helpful for readers to follow up on this (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Today, Bertalanffy's GST remains a bridge for interdisciplinary study of systems in the social sciences.</p>
