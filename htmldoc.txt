<p><b>Hysteresis</b> is the dependence of the state of a system on its history. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnet</a> may have more than one possible <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_moment" title="Magnetic moment">magnetic moment</a> in a given <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>, depending on how the field changed in the past. Plots of a single component of the moment often form a loop or hysteresis curve, where there are different values of one variable depending on the direction of change of another variable. This history dependence is the basis of memory in a <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">hard disk drive</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Remanence" title="Remanence">remanence</a> that retains a record of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a> in the past. Hysteresis occurs in <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferroelectricity" title="Ferroelectricity">ferroelectric</a> materials, as well as in the <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics)" title="Deformation (mechanics)">deformation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rubber_band" title="Rubber band">rubber bands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shape-memory_alloy" title="Shape-memory alloy">shape-memory alloys</a> and many other natural phenomena. In natural systems it is often associated with <a href="/wiki/Irreversible_process" title="Irreversible process">irreversible thermodynamic change</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Phase_transitions" class="mw-redirect" title="Phase transitions">phase transitions</a> and with <a href="/wiki/Internal_friction" class="mw-redirect" title="Internal friction">internal friction</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipation</a> is a common side effect.</p>
<p>Hysteresis can be found in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, and so on. It is incorporated in many artificial systems: for example, in <a href="/wiki/Thermostat" title="Thermostat">thermostats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schmitt_trigger" title="Schmitt trigger">Schmitt triggers</a>, it prevents unwanted frequent switching.</p>
<p>Hysteresis can be a dynamic <a href="/wiki/Lag" title="Lag">lag</a> between an input and an output that disappears if the input is varied more slowly; this is known as <i>rate-dependent</i> hysteresis. However, phenomena such as the magnetic hysteresis loops are mainly <i>rate-independent</i>, which makes possible a durable memory.</p>
<p>Systems with hysteresis are <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_system" title="Nonlinear system">nonlinear</a>, and can be mathematically challenging to model. Some models such as the <a href="/wiki/Preisach_model_of_hysteresis" title="Preisach model of hysteresis">Preisach model</a> (originally applied to ferromagnetism) and the <a href="/wiki/Bouc%E2%80%93Wen_model_of_hysteresis" title="Boucâ€“Wen model of hysteresis">Bouc-Wen model</a> attempt to capture general features of hysteresis; and there are also phenomenological models for particular phenomena such as the <a href="/wiki/Jiles-Atherton_model" title="Jiles-Atherton model">Jiles-Atherton model</a> for ferromagnetism.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_history">Etymology and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "hysteresis" is derived from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">á½‘ÏƒÏ„Î­Ï�Î·ÏƒÎ¹Ï‚</span>, an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> word meaning "deficiency" or "lagging behind". It was coined around 1890 by <a href="/wiki/James_Alfred_Ewing" title="James Alfred Ewing">Sir James Alfred Ewing</a> to describe the behaviour of magnetic materials.</p>
<p>Some early work on describing hysteresis in mechanical systems was performed by <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a>. Subsequently, hysteretic models have received significant attention in the works of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ferenc_Preisach&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ferenc Preisach (page does not exist)">Ferenc Preisach</a> (<a href="/wiki/Preisach_model_of_hysteresis" title="Preisach model of hysteresis">Preisach model of hysteresis</a>), <a href="/wiki/Louis_N%C3%A9el" title="Louis NÃ©el">Louis NÃ©el</a> and D. H. Everett in connection with magnetism and absorption. A more formal mathematical theory of systems with hysteresis was developed in the 1970s by a group of Russian mathematicians led by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Krasnosel%27skii" title="Mark Krasnosel'skii">Mark Krasnosel'skii</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayergoyz2003_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayergoyz2003-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rate-independent">Rate-independent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rate-independent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Systems with <i>rate-independent hysteresis</i> have a <i>persistent</i> memory of the past that remains after the transients have died out.<sup id="cite_ref-rate_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rate-3">[3]</a></sup> The future development of such a system depends on the history of states visited, but does not fade as the events recede into the past. If an input variable <span class="texhtml"><var>X(t)</var></span> cycles from <span class="texhtml"><var>X</var><sub>0</sub></span> to <span class="texhtml"><var>X</var><sub>1</sub></span> and back again, the output <span class="texhtml"><var>Y(t)</var></span> may be <span class="texhtml"><var>Y</var><sub>0</sub></span> initially but a different value <span class="texhtml"><var>Y</var><sub>2</sub></span> upon return. The values of <span class="texhtml"><var>Y(t)</var></span> depend on the path of values that <span class="texhtml"><var>X(t)</var></span> passes through but not on the speed at which it traverses the path.<sup id="cite_ref-Bertotti1998ch2_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bertotti1998ch2-2">[2]</a></sup> Many authors restrict the term hysteresis to mean only rate-independent hysteresis.<sup id="cite_ref-Visintin1994p13_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Visintin1994p13-4">[4]</a></sup> Hysteresis effects can be characterized using the <a href="/wiki/Preisach_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Preisach model">Preisach model</a> and the generalized <a href="/w/index.php?title=Prandtl-Ishlinskii_model&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Prandtl-Ishlinskii model (page does not exist)">Prandtl-Ishlinskii model</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_engineering">In engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Control_systems">Control systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Control systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In control systems, hysteresis can be used to filter signals so that the output reacts less rapidly than it otherwise would, by taking recent history into account. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Thermostat" title="Thermostat">thermostat</a> controlling a heater may switch the heater on when the temperature drops below A, but not turn it off until the temperature rises above B. (For instance, if one wishes to maintain a temperature of 20&#160;Â°C then one might set the thermostat to turn the heater on when the temperature drops to below 18&#160;Â°C and off when the temperature exceeds 22&#160;Â°C.)</p>
<p>Similarly, a pressure switch can be designed to exhibit hysteresis, with pressure set-points substituted for temperature thresholds.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic_circuits">Electronic circuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Electronic circuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Often, some amount of hysteresis is intentionally added to an electronic circuit to prevent unwanted rapid switching. This and similar techniques are used to compensate for <a href="/wiki/Switch#Contact_bounce" title="Switch">contact bounce</a> in switches, or <a href="/wiki/Noise_(electronic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Noise (electronic)">noise</a> in an electrical signal.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Schmitt_trigger" title="Schmitt trigger">Schmitt trigger</a> is a simple electronic circuit that exhibits this property.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Relay#Latching_relay" title="Relay">latching relay</a> uses a solenoid to actuate a ratcheting mechanism that keeps the relay closed even if power to the relay is terminated.</p>
<p>Hysteresis is essential to the workings of some <a href="/wiki/Memristor" title="Memristor">memristors</a> (circuit components which "remember" changes in the current passing through them by changing their resistance).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hysteresis can be used when connecting arrays of elements such as <a href="/wiki/Nanoelectronic" class="mw-redirect" title="Nanoelectronic">nanoelectronics</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrochrome_cells&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Electrochrome cells (page does not exist)">electrochrome cells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Memory_effect" title="Memory effect">memory effect</a> devices using <a href="/wiki/Passive_matrix_addressing" title="Passive matrix addressing">passive matrix addressing</a>. Shortcuts are made between adjacent components (see <a href="/wiki/Crosstalk" title="Crosstalk">crosstalk</a>) and the hysteresis helps to keep the components in a particular state while the other components change states. Thus, all rows can be addressed at the same time instead of individually.</p>
<p>In the field of audio electronics, a <a href="/wiki/Noise_gate" title="Noise gate">noise gate</a> often implements hysteresis intentionally to prevent the gate from "chattering" when signals close to its threshold are applied.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="User_interface_design">User interface design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: User interface design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A hysteresis is sometimes intentionally added to computer algorithms. The field of <a href="/wiki/User_interface_design" title="User interface design">user interface design</a> has borrowed the term hysteresis to refer to times when the state of the user interface intentionally lags behind the apparent user input. For example, a menu that was drawn in response to a mouse-over event may remain on-screen for a brief moment after the mouse has moved out of the trigger region and the menu region. This allows the user to move the mouse directly to an item on the menu, even if part of that direct mouse path is outside of both the trigger region and the menu region. For instance, right-clicking on the desktop in most Windows interfaces will create a menu that exhibits this behavior.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerodynamics">Aerodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Aerodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In aerodynamics, hysteresis can be observed when decreasing the angle of attack of a wing after stall, regarding the lift and drag coefficients. The angle of attack where the flow on top of the wing reattaches is generally lower than the angle of attack where the flow separates during the increase of the angle of attack.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_mechanics">In mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elastic_hysteresis">Elastic hysteresis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Elastic hysteresis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Elastic_Hysteresis.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Elastic_Hysteresis.svg/220px-Elastic_Hysteresis.svg.png" width="220" height="189" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Elastic_Hysteresis.svg/330px-Elastic_Hysteresis.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Elastic_Hysteresis.svg/440px-Elastic_Hysteresis.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="595" data-file-height="512" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Elastic_Hysteresis.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Elastic hysteresis of an idealized rubber band. The area in the centre of the hysteresis loop is the energy dissipated due to internal friction.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the elastic hysteresis of rubber, the area in the centre of a hysteresis loop is the energy dissipated due to material <a href="/wiki/Internal_friction" class="mw-redirect" title="Internal friction">internal friction</a>.</p>
<p>Elastic hysteresis was one of the first types of hysteresis to be examined.<sup id="cite_ref-LoveTreatise_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LoveTreatise-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ewing_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ewing-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A simple way to understand it is in terms of a rubber band with weights attached to it. If the top of a rubber band is hung on a hook and small weights are attached to the bottom of the band one at a time, it will get longer. As more weights are <i>loaded</i> onto it, the band will continue to extend because the force the weights are exerting on the band is increasing. When each weight is taken off, or <i>unloaded</i>, the band will get shorter as the force is reduced. As the weights are taken off, each weight that produced a specific length as it was loaded onto the band now produces a slightly longer length as it is unloaded. This is because the band does not obey <a href="/wiki/Hooke%27s_law" title="Hooke's law">Hooke's law</a> perfectly. The hysteresis loop of an idealized rubber band is shown in the figure.</p>
<p>In terms of force, the rubber band was harder to stretch when it was being loaded than when it was being unloaded. In terms of time, when the band is unloaded, the effect (the length) lagged behind the cause(the force of the weights) because the length has not yet reached the value it had for the same weight during the loading part of the cycle. In terms of energy, more energy was required during the loading than the unloading, the excess energy being dissipated as heat.</p>
<p>Elastic hysteresis is more pronounced when the loading and unloading is done quickly than when it is done slowly.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Some materials such as hard metals don't show elastic hysteresis under a moderate load, whereas other hard materials like granite and marble do. Materials such as rubber exhibit a high degree of elastic hysteresis.</p>
<p>When the intrinsic hysteresis of rubber is being measured, the material can be considered to behave like a gas. When a rubber band is stretched it heats up, and if it is suddenly released, it cools down perceptibly. These effects correspond to a large hysteresis from the thermal exchange with the environment and a smaller hysteresis due to internal friction within the rubber. This proper, intrinsic hysteresis can be measured only if the rubber band is adiabatically isolated.</p>
<p>Small vehicle suspensions using <a href="/wiki/Rubber" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubber">rubber</a> (or other <a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">elastomers</a>) can achieve the dual function of springing and damping because rubber, unlike metal springs, has pronounced hysteresis and does not return all the absorbed compression energy on the rebound. <a href="/wiki/Mountain_bike" title="Mountain bike">Mountain bikes</a> have made use of elastomer suspension, as did the original <a href="/wiki/Mini" title="Mini">Mini</a> car.</p>
<p>The primary cause of <a href="/wiki/Rolling_resistance" title="Rolling resistance">rolling resistance</a> when a body (such as a ball, tire, or wheel) rolls on a surface is hysteresis. This is attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Viscoelasticity" title="Viscoelasticity">viscoelastic characteristics</a> of the material of the rolling body.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contact_angle_hysteresis">Contact angle hysteresis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Contact angle hysteresis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Contact_angle" title="Contact angle">contact angle</a> formed between a liquid and solid phase will exhibit a range of contact angles that are possible. There are two common methods for measuring this range of contact angles. The first method is referred to as the tilting base method. Once a drop is dispensed on the surface with the surface level, the surface is then tilted from 0Â° to 90Â°. As the drop is tilted, the downhill side will be in a state of imminent wetting while the uphill side will be in a state of imminent dewetting. As the tilt increases the downhill contact angle will increase and represents the advancing contact angle while the uphill side will decrease; this is the receding contact angle. The values for these angles just prior to the drop releasing will typically represent the advancing and receding contact angles. The difference between these two angles is the contact angle hysteresis.</p>
<p>The second method is often referred to as the add/remove volume method. When the maximum liquid volume is removed from the drop without the <a href="/wiki/Interfacial_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Interfacial area">interfacial area</a> decreasing the receding contact angle is thus measured. When volume is added to the maximum before the interfacial area increases, this is the <a href="/wiki/Advancing_contact_angle" class="mw-redirect" title="Advancing contact angle">advancing contact angle</a>. As with the tilt method, the difference between the advancing and receding contact angles is the contact angle hysteresis. Most researchers prefer the tilt method; the add/remove method requires that a tip or needle stay embedded in the drop which can affect the accuracy of the values, especially the receding contact angle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bubble_shape_hysteresis">Bubble shape hysteresis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bubble shape hysteresis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The equilibrium shapes of <a href="/wiki/Bubble_(physics)" title="Bubble (physics)">bubbles</a> expanding and contracting on capillaries (<a href="/wiki/Hypodermic_needle#Non-specialist_use" title="Hypodermic needle">blunt needles</a>) can exhibit hysteresis depending on the relative magnitude of the <a href="/wiki/Maximum_bubble_pressure_method#Maximum_bubble_pressure_method" title="Maximum bubble pressure method">maximum capillary pressure</a> to ambient pressure, and the relative magnitude of the bubble volume at the maximum capillary pressure to the dead volume in the system.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> The bubble shape hysteresis is a consequence of gas <a href="/wiki/Compressibility" title="Compressibility">compressibility</a>, which causes the bubbles to behave differently across expansion and contraction. During expansion, bubbles undergo large non equilibrium jumps in volume, while during contraction the bubbles are more stable and undergo a relatively smaller jump in volume resulting in an asymmetry across expansion and contraction. The bubble shape hysteresis is qualitatively similar to the adsorption hysteresis, and as in the contact angle hysteresis, the interfacial properties play an important role in bubble shape hysteresis.</p>
<p>The existence of the bubble shape hysteresis has important consequences in <a href="/wiki/Surface_rheology" title="Surface rheology">interfacial rheology</a> experiments involving bubbles. As a result of the hysteresis, not all sizes of the bubbles can be formed on a capillary. Further the gas compressibility causing the hysteresis leads to unintended complications in the phase relation between the applied changes in interfacial area to the expected interfacial stresses. These difficulties can be avoided by designing experimental systems to avoid the bubble shape hysteresis.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adsorption_hysteresis">Adsorption hysteresis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Adsorption hysteresis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hysteresis can also occur during physical <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> processes. In this type of hysteresis, the quantity adsorbed is different when gas is being added than it is when being removed. The specific causes of adsorption hysteresis are still an active area of research, but it is linked to differences in the nucleation and evaporation mechanisms inside mesopores. These mechanisms are further complicated by effects such as <a href="/wiki/Cavitation" title="Cavitation">cavitation</a> and pore blocking.</p>
<p>In physical adsorption, hysteresis is evidence of <a href="/wiki/Mesoporosity" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesoporosity">mesoporosity</a>-indeed, the definition of mesopores (2â€“50&#160;nm) is associated with the appearance (50&#160;nm) and disappearance (2&#160;nm) of mesoporosity in nitrogen adsorption isotherms as a function of Kelvin radius.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> An adsorption isotherm showing hysteresis is said to be of Type IV (for a wetting adsorbate) or Type V (for a non-wetting adsorbate), and hysteresis loops themselves are classified according to how symmetric the loop is.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Adsorption hysteresis loops also have the unusual property that it is possible to scan within a hysteresis loop by reversing the direction of adsorption while on a point on the loop. The resulting scans are called "crossing," "converging," or "returning," depending on the shape of the isotherm at this point.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Matric_potential_hysteresis">Matric potential hysteresis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Matric potential hysteresis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The relationship between matric <a href="/wiki/Water_potential" title="Water potential">water potential</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_content" title="Water content">water content</a> is the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Water_retention_curve" title="Water retention curve">water retention curve</a>. <a href="/wiki/Matric_potential" class="mw-redirect" title="Matric potential">Matric potential</a> measurements (Î¨<sub>m</sub>) are converted to volumetric water content (Î¸) measurements based on a site or soil specific calibration curve. Hysteresis is a source of water content measurement error. Matric potential hysteresis arises from differences in wetting behaviour causing dry medium to re-wet; that is, it depends on the saturation history of the porous medium. Hysteretic behaviour means that, for example, at a matric potential (Î¨<sub>m</sub>) of <span class="nowrap">5 kPa</span>, the volumetric water content (Î¸) of a fine sandy soil matrix could be anything between 8% to 25%.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tensiometer_(soil_science)" title="Tensiometer (soil science)">Tensiometers</a> are directly influenced by this type of hysteresis. Two other types of sensors used to measure soil water matric potential are also influenced by hysteresis effects within the sensor itself. Resistance blocks, both nylon and gypsum based, measure matric potential as a function of electrical resistance. The relation between the sensorâ€™s electrical resistance and sensor matric potential is hysteretic. Thermocouples measure matric potential as a function of heat dissipation. Hysteresis occurs because measured heat dissipation depends on sensor water content, and the sensor water contentâ€“matric potential relationship is hysteretic. As of 2002<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, only desorption curves are usually measured during calibration of <a href="/wiki/Soil_moisture_sensors" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil moisture sensors">soil moisture sensors</a>. Despite the fact that it can be a source of significant error, the sensor specific effect of hysteresis is generally ignored.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_materials">In materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: In materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_hysteresis">Magnetic hysteresis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Magnetic hysteresis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_hysteresis" title="Magnetic hysteresis">Magnetic hysteresis</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:StonerWohlfarthMainLoop.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/StonerWohlfarthMainLoop.svg/400px-StonerWohlfarthMainLoop.svg.png" width="400" height="352" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/StonerWohlfarthMainLoop.svg/600px-StonerWohlfarthMainLoop.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/StonerWohlfarthMainLoop.svg/800px-StonerWohlfarthMainLoop.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="451" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<a href="/wiki/Stoner%E2%80%93Wohlfarth_model" title="Stonerâ€“Wohlfarth model">Theoretical model</a> of <a href="/wiki/Magnetization" title="Magnetization">magnetization</a> <span class="texhtml"><var>m</var></span> against <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> <span class="texhtml"><var>h</var></span>. Starting at the origin, the upward curve is the <i>initial magnetization curve</i>. The downward curve after saturation, along with the lower return curve, form the <i>main loop</i>. The intercepts <span class="texhtml"><var>h</var><sub>c</sub></span> and <span class="texhtml"><var>m</var><sub>rs</sub></span> are the <i><a href="/wiki/Coercivity" title="Coercivity">coercivity</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Remanence#Saturation_remanence" title="Remanence">saturation remanence</a></i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When an external <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> is applied to a <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetic material</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, the atomic <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipoles</a> align themselves with it. Even when the field is removed, part of the alignment will be retained: the material has become <i>magnetized</i>. Once magnetized, the magnet will stay magnetized indefinitely. To <a href="/wiki/Magnet#Demagnetizing_ferromagnets" title="Magnet">demagnetize</a> it requires heat or a magnetic field in the opposite direction. This is the effect that provides the element of memory in a <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">hard disk drive</a>.</p>
<p>The relationship between field strength <span class="texhtml"><var>H</var></span> and magnetization <span class="texhtml"><var>M</var></span> is not linear in such materials. If a magnet is demagnetized (<span class="texhtml"><var>H=M=0</var></span>) and the relationship between <span class="texhtml"><var>H</var></span> and <span class="texhtml"><var>M</var></span> is plotted for increasing levels of field strength, <span class="texhtml"><var>M</var></span> follows the <i>initial magnetization curve</i>. This curve increases rapidly at first and then approaches an <a href="/wiki/Asymptote" title="Asymptote">asymptote</a> called <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(magnetic)" title="Saturation (magnetic)">magnetic saturation</a>. If the magnetic field is now reduced monotonically, <span class="texhtml"><var>M</var></span> follows a different curve. At zero field strength, the magnetization is offset from the origin by an amount called the <a href="/wiki/Remanence" title="Remanence">remanence</a>. If the <span class="texhtml"><var>H-M</var></span> relationship is plotted for all strengths of applied magnetic field the result is a hysteresis loop called the <i>main loop</i>. The width of the middle section is twice the <a href="/wiki/Coercivity" title="Coercivity">coercivity</a> of the material.<sup id="cite_ref-Chikazumi1997ch1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chikazumi1997ch1-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>A closer look at a magnetization curve generally reveals a series of small, random jumps in magnetization called <a href="/wiki/Barkhausen_effect" title="Barkhausen effect">Barkhausen jumps</a>. This effect is due to <a href="/wiki/Crystallographic_defect" title="Crystallographic defect">crystallographic defects</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Dislocation" title="Dislocation">dislocations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chikazumi1997ch15_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chikazumi1997ch15-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Magnetic hysteresis loops are not exclusive to materials with ferromagnetic ordering. Other magnetic orderings, such as <a href="/wiki/Spin_glass" title="Spin glass">spin glass</a> ordering, also exhibit this phenomena.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_origin">Physical origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Physical origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">Ferromagnetism</a></div>
<p>The phenomenon of hysteresis in <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetic</a> materials is the result of two effects: rotation of <a href="/wiki/Magnetization" title="Magnetization">magnetization</a> and changes in size or number of <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_domain" title="Magnetic domain">magnetic domains</a>. In general, the magnetization varies (in direction but not magnitude) across a magnet, but in sufficiently small magnets, it does not. In these <a href="/wiki/Single_domain_(magnetic)" title="Single domain (magnetic)">single-domain</a> magnets, the magnetization responds to a magnetic field by rotating. Single-domain magnets are used wherever a strong, stable magnetization is needed (for example, <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_recording" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetic recording">magnetic recording</a>).</p>
<p>Larger magnets are divided into regions called <i>domains</i>. Across each domain, the magnetization does not vary; but between domains are relatively thin <i>domain walls</i> in which the direction of magnetization rotates from the direction of one domain to another. If the magnetic field changes, the walls move, changing the relative sizes of the domains. Because the domains are not magnetized in the same direction, the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_moment" title="Magnetic moment">magnetic moment</a> per unit volume is smaller than it would be in a single-domain magnet; but domain walls involve rotation of only a small part of the magnetization, so it is much easier to change the magnetic moment. The magnetization can also change by addition or subtraction of domains (called <i>nucleation</i> and <i>denucleation</i>).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_hysteresis_models">Magnetic hysteresis models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Magnetic hysteresis models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The most known empirical models in hysteresis are <a href="/wiki/Preisach_model_of_hysteresis" title="Preisach model of hysteresis">Preisach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jiles-Atherton_model" title="Jiles-Atherton model">Jiles-Atherton models</a>. These models allow an accurate modeling of the hysteresis loop and are widely used in the industry. However, these models lose the connection with thermodynamics and the energy consistency is not ensured. Last models rely on a consistent thermodynamic formulation. VINCH model<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> is inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Work_hardening" title="Work hardening">kinematic hardening</a> laws and by the <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Irreversible_process" title="Irreversible process">irreversible processes</a>. In particular, in addition to provide an accurate modeling, the stored magnetic energy and the dissipated energy are known at all times. The obtained incremental formulation is variationally consistent, i.e., all internal variables follow from the minimization of a thermodynamic potential. That allows to obtain easily a vectorial model while Preisach and Jiles-Atherton are fundamentally scalar models.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magnet#Common_uses_of_magnets" title="Magnet">Magnet Â§&#160;Common uses of magnets</a></div>
<p>There are a great variety of applications of the hysteresis in ferromagnets. Many of these make use of their ability to retain a memory, for example <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">magnetic tape</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hard_disks" class="mw-redirect" title="Hard disks">hard disks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Credit_card" title="Credit card">credit cards</a>. In these applications, <i>hard</i> magnets (high coercivity) like <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> are desirable so the memory is not easily erased.</p>
<p><i>Soft</i> magnets (low coercivity) are used as cores in <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">electromagnets</a>. The nonlinear response of the magnetic moment to a magnetic field boosts the response of the coil wrapped around it. The low coercivity reduces that energy loss associated with hysteresis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_hysteresis">Electrical hysteresis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Electrical hysteresis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Electrical hysteresis typically occurs in <a href="/wiki/Ferroelectric" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferroelectric">ferroelectric</a> material, where domains of polarization contribute to the total polarization. Polarization is the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_dipole_moment" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical dipole moment">electrical dipole moment</a> (either <a href="/wiki/Coulomb" title="Coulomb">C</a>Â·<a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a><sup>âˆ’2</sup> or <a href="/wiki/Coulomb" title="Coulomb">C</a>Â·<a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a>). The mechanism, an organization of the polarization into domains, is similar to that of magnetic hysteresis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid.E2.80.93solid-phase_transitions">Liquidâ€“solid-phase transitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Liquidâ€“solid-phase transitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hysteresis manifests itself in state transitions when <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting temperature</a> and freezing temperature do not agree. For example, <a href="/wiki/Agar" title="Agar">agar</a> melts at 85 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Â°C</a> and solidifies from 32 to 40&#160;Â°C. This is to say that once agar is melted at 85&#160;Â°C, it retains a liquid state until cooled to 40&#160;Â°C. Therefore, from the temperatures of 40 to 85&#160;Â°C, agar can be either solid or liquid, depending on which state it was before.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_biology">In biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: In biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell_biology_and_genetics">Cell biology and genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Cell biology and genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">Cell biology</a></div>
<p>Hysteresis in cell biology often follows <a href="/wiki/Bistability#In_biological_and_chemical_systems" title="Bistability">bistable systems</a> where the same input state can lead to two different, stable outputs. Where bistability can lead to digital, switch-like outputs from the continuous inputs of chemical concentrations and activities, hysteresis makes these systems more resistant to noise. These systems are often characterized by higher values of the input required to switch into a particular state as compared to the input required to stay in the state, allowing for a transition that is not continuously reversible, and thus less susceptible to noise.</p>
<p>Cells undergoing <a href="/wiki/Cell_division" title="Cell division">cell division</a> exhibit hysteresis in that it takes a higher concentration of <a href="/wiki/Cyclin" title="Cyclin">cyclins</a> to switch them from G2 phase into <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a> than to stay in mitosis once begun.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biochemical systems can also show hysteresis-like output when slowly varying states that are not directly monitored are involved, as in the case of the cell cycle arrest in yeast in exposed to mating pheromone.<sup id="cite_ref-Doncic_2013_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doncic_2013-24">[24]</a></sup> Here, the duration of cell cycle arrest depends not only on the final level of input Fus3, but also on the previously achieved Fus3 levels. This effect is achieved in due to the slower time scales involved in the transcription of intermediate Far1, such that the total Far1 activity reaches its equilibrium value slowly, and for transient changes in Fus3 concentration, the response of the system depends on the Far1 concentration achieved with the transient value. Experiments in this type of hysteresis benefit from the ability to change the concentration of the inputs with time. The mechanisms are often elucidated by allowing independent control of the concentration of the key intermediate, for instance, by using an inducible promoter.</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chromatin" title="Chromatin">Chromatin</a></div>
<p>Darlington in his classic works on <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> discussed hysteresis of the <a href="/wiki/Chromosomes" class="mw-redirect" title="Chromosomes">chromosomes</a>, by which he meant "failure of the external form of the chromosomes to respond immediately to the internal stresses due to changes in their molecular spiral", as they lie in a somewhat rigid medium in the limited space of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a>.</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Morphogen" title="Morphogen">Morphogen</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">developmental biology</a>, cell type diversity is regulated by long range-acting signaling molecules called <a href="/wiki/Morphogens" class="mw-redirect" title="Morphogens">morphogens</a> that pattern uniform pools of cells in a concentration- and time-dependent manner. The morphogen <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Hedgehog" class="mw-redirect" title="Sonic Hedgehog">Sonic Hedgehog</a> (Shh), for example, acts on <a href="/wiki/Limb_bud" title="Limb bud">limb bud</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neural_progenitors&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Neural progenitors (page does not exist)">neural progenitors</a> to induce expression of a set of <a href="/wiki/Homeodomain" class="mw-redirect" title="Homeodomain">homeodomain</a>-containing <a href="/wiki/Transcription_factors" class="mw-redirect" title="Transcription factors">transcription factors</a> to subdivide these tissues into distinct domains. It has been shown that these tissues have a 'memory' of previous exposure to Shh.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In neural tissue, this hysteresis is regulated by a homeodomain (HD) feedback circuit that amplifies Shh signaling.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In this circuit, expression of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gli (page does not exist)">Gli</a> transcription factors, the executors of the Shh pathway, is suppressed. Glis are processed to repressor forms (GliR) in the absence of Shh, but in the presence of Shh, a proportion of Glis are maintained as full-length proteins allowed to translocate to the nucleus, where they act as activators (GliA) of transcription. By reducing Gli expression then, the HD transcription factors reduce the total amount of Gli (GliT), so a higher proportion of GliT can be stabilized as GliA for the same concentration of Shh.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Immunology">Immunology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Immunology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is some evidence that T cells exhibit hysteresis in that it takes a lower signal threshold to activate T cells that have been previously activated. Ras activation is required for downstream effector functions of activated T cells.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Triggering of the T cell receptor induces high levels of Ras activation, which results in higher levels of GTP-bound (active) Ras at the cell surface. Since higher levels of active Ras have accumulated at the cell surface in T cells that have been previously stimulated by strong engagement of the T cell receptor, weaker subsequent T cell receptor signals received shortly afterwards will deliver the same level of activation due to the presence of higher levels of already activated Ras as compared to a naÃ¯ve cell.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neuroscience">Neuroscience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Neuroscience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Refractory_period_(physiology)" title="Refractory period (physiology)">Refractory period (physiology)</a></div>
<p>The property by which some <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a> do not return to their basal conditions from a stimulated condition immediately after removal of the stimulus is an example of hysteresis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Respiratory_physiology">Respiratory physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Respiratory physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lung hysteresis is evident when observing the compliance of a lung on inspiration versus expiration. The difference in compliance (volume/pressure) is due to the additional energy required during inspiration to recruit and inflate additional alveoli.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Transpulmonary_pressure" title="Transpulmonary pressure">transpulmonary pressure</a> vs Volume curve of inhalation is different from the Pressure vs Volume curve of exhalation, the difference being described as hysteresis. Lung volume at any given pressure during inhalation is less than the lung volume at any given pressure during exhalation.<sup id="cite_ref-isbn0-7817-5152-7_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7817-5152-7-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice_and_speech_physiology">Voice and speech physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Voice and speech physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A hysteresis effect may be observed in voicing onset versus offset.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The threshold value of the subglottal pressure required to start the vocal fold vibration is lower than the threshold value at which the vibration stops, when other parameters are kept constant. In utterances of vowel-voiceless consonant-vowel sequences during speech, the intraoral pressure is lower at the voice onset of the second vowel compared to the voice offset of the first vowel, the oral airflow is lower, the transglottal pressure is larger and the glottal width is smaller.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_epidemiology">Ecology and epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Ecology and epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hysteresis is a commonly encountered phenomenon in ecology and epidemiology, where the observed equilibrium of a system can not be predicted solely based on environmental variables, but also requires knowledge of the system's past history. Notable examples include the theory of spruce budworm outbreaks and behavioral-effects on disease transmission.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_economics">In economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: In economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hysteresis_(economics)" title="Hysteresis (economics)">Hysteresis (economics)</a></div>
<p>Economic systems can exhibit hysteresis. For example, <a href="/wiki/Export" title="Export">export</a> performance is subject to strong hysteresis effects: because of the fixed transportation costs it may take a big push to start a country's exports, but once the transition is made, not much may be required to keep them going.</p>
<p>When some negative shock reduces employment in a company or industry, fewer employed workers then remain. As usually the employed workers have the power to set wages, their reduced number incentivizes them to bargain for even higher wages when the economy again gets better instead of letting the wage be at the <a href="/wiki/Equilibrium_wage" class="mw-redirect" title="Equilibrium wage">equilibrium wage</a> level, where the supply and demand of workers would match. This causes hysteresis: the unemployment becomes permanently higher after negative shocks.<sup id="cite_ref-Ball_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanently_higher_unemployment">Permanently higher unemployment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Permanently higher unemployment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The idea of hysteresis is used extensively in the area of labor economics, specifically with reference to the <a href="/wiki/Unemployment_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Unemployment rate">unemployment rate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hargreaves-Heap_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hargreaves-Heap-35">[35]</a></sup> According to theories based on hysteresis, severe economic downturns (recession) and/or persistent stagnation (slow demand growth, usually after a recession) cause unemployed individuals to lose their job skills (commonly developed on the job) or to find them become obsolete or become demotivated/disillusioned/depressed or lose job-seeking skills. In addition, employers may use time spent in unemployment as a screening tool, i.e., to weed out less desired employees in hiring decisions. Then, in times of an economic upturn, recovery, or 'boom', the affected workers will not share in the prosperity, remaining unemployed for long periods (e.g., over 52 weeks). This makes unemployment "structural", i.e., extremely difficult to reduce simply by increasing the aggregate demand for products and labor without causing increased inflation. That is, it is possible that a <a href="/wiki/Ratchet_effect" title="Ratchet effect">ratchet effect</a> in unemployment rates exists, so a short-term rise in unemployment rates tend to persist. For example, traditional anti-inflationary policy (the use of recession to fight inflation) leads to a permanently higher 'natural' rate of unemployment (more scientifically known as the <a href="/wiki/NAIRU" title="NAIRU">NAIRU</a>). This occurs first because inflationary expectations are '<a href="/wiki/Sticky_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sticky (economics)">sticky</a>' downward due to wage and price rigidities (and so adapt slowly over time rather than being approximately correct as in theories of <a href="/wiki/Rational_expectations" title="Rational expectations">rational expectations</a>) and second because labor markets do not clear instantly in response to unemployment.</p>
<p>The existence of hysteresis has been put forward as a possible explanation for the persistently high unemployment of many economies in the 1990s. Hysteresis has been invoked by <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Blanchard" title="Olivier Blanchard">Olivier Blanchard</a> among others to explain the differences in long run unemployment rates between Europe and the United States. Labor market reform (usually meaning institutional change promoting more flexible wages, firing, and hiring) or strong demand-side economic growth may not therefore reduce this pool of long-term unemployed. Thus, specific targeted training programs are presented as a possible policy solution.<sup id="cite_ref-Ball_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball-33">[33]</a></sup> However, the hysteresis hypothesis suggests such training programs are aided by persistently high demand for products (perhaps with <a href="/wiki/Incomes_policies" class="mw-redirect" title="Incomes policies">incomes policies</a> to avoid increased inflation), which reduces the transition costs out of unemployment and into paid employment easier.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Game_theory">Game theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Game theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hysteresis occurs in applications of <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a> to economics, in models with product quality, agent honesty or corruption of various institutions. Slightly different initial conditions can lead to opposite outcomes and resulting stable good and bad <a href="/wiki/Correlated_equilibrium" title="Correlated equilibrium">equilibria</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_considerations">Additional considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Additional considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Models_of_hysteresis">Models of hysteresis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Models of hysteresis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Each subject that involves hysteresis has models that are specific to the subject. In addition, there are models that capture general features of many systems with hysteresis.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayergoyz2003_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayergoyz2003-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-model1995_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-model1995-36">[36]</a></sup> An example is the <a href="/wiki/Preisach_model_of_hysteresis" title="Preisach model of hysteresis">Preisach model of hysteresis</a>, which represents a hysteresis nonlinearity as a <a href="/wiki/Linear_superposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Linear superposition">linear superposition</a> of square loops called non-ideal relays.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayergoyz2003_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayergoyz2003-1">[1]</a></sup> Many complex models of hysteresis arise from the simple parallel connection, or superposition, of elementary carriers of hysteresis termed hysterons.</p>
<p>A simple parametric description of various hysteretic loops may be found in the Lapshin model of hysteresis.<sup id="cite_ref-model1995_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-model1995-36">[36]</a></sup> Along with the classical loop (see figure at the top of the page), substitution of trapezoidal or triangle pulses instead of the harmonic functions also allows piecewise-linear hysteresis loops (see <a href="/wiki/Hysteresis#Electronic_circuits" title="Hysteresis">Electronic circuit example</a>) frequently used in discrete automatics to be built in the model. There is an implementation of the hysteresis model in <a href="/wiki/R_(programming_language)" title="R (programming language)">R programming language</a> (package Hysteresis).<sup id="cite_ref-PackHyst_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PackHyst-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bouc%E2%80%93Wen_model_of_hysteresis" title="Boucâ€“Wen model of hysteresis">Boucâ€“Wen model of hysteresis</a> is often used to describe non-linear hysteretic systems. It was introduced by Bouc<sup id="cite_ref-Bouc67_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bouc67-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bouc71_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bouc71-39">[39]</a></sup> and extended by Wen,<sup id="cite_ref-Wen76_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wen76-40">[40]</a></sup> who demonstrated its versatility by producing a variety of hysteretic patterns. This model is able to capture in analytical form, a range of shapes of hysteretic cycles which match the behaviour of a wide class of hysteretical systems; therefore, given its versability and mathematical tractability, the Boucâ€“Wen model has quickly gained popularity and has been extended and applied to a wide variety of engineering problems, including multi-degree-of-freedom (MDOF) systems, buildings, frames, bidirectional and <a href="/wiki/Torsion_(mechanics)" title="Torsion (mechanics)">torsional</a> response of hysteretic systems two- and three-dimensional continua, and <a href="/wiki/Soil_liquefaction" title="Soil liquefaction">soil liquefaction</a> among others. The Boucâ€“Wen model and its variants/extensions have been used in applications of <a href="/wiki/Structural_engineering" title="Structural engineering">structural control</a>, in particular in the modeling of the behaviour of <a href="/wiki/Magnetorheological_damper" title="Magnetorheological damper">magnetorheological dampers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Base_isolation" title="Base isolation">base isolation</a> devices for buildings and other kinds of damping devices; it has also been in the modelling and analysis of structures built of reinforced concrete, steel, masonry and timber.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hysteresis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When hysteresis occurs with <a href="/wiki/Intensive_and_extensive_properties" title="Intensive and extensive properties">extensive and intensive variables</a>, the work done on the system is the area under the hysteresis graph.</p>
